Local government is predicated on the assumption of neutrality. Constituents grant authority to elected officials with the expectation that public assets, housing standards, and financial budgets will be managed solely in the public interest.

However, a forensic review of the Lewisham Council Register of Interests, cross-referenced with public regulatory findings and corporate development yields, paints a fundamentally different picture.

Lewisham Council is currently operating under a state of systematic institutional capture. This is not a matter of illegal cash exchanges in dark rooms; it is a highly sanitised, normalised infrastructure where the boundaries between public duty, private equity, trade union payrolls, and national party machinery have completely dissolved.

The following report details the specific individuals, the structural conflicts, and the receipts of this administrative failure — and a manifesto to reclaim what has been lost.

I. Marking Their Own Homework: The Audit Conflict

The Audit and Risk Committee is the final line of defence against financial mismanagement. Its purpose is to scrutinise the Council's internal controls, evaluate risk, and ensure taxpayer money is not being misappropriated or mismanaged. Absolute financial independence of its members is mandatory for public trust.

In October 2024, the Housing Ombudsman published a devastating investigation into Lewisham Council, citing a 92% maladministration rate and highlighting "severe maladministration" regarding damp, leaks, and pest infestations. For the Council to permit a landlord on its own payroll to chair its Audit Committee demonstrates a total abandonment of administrative rigour.

Subject: Councillor Eva Kestner
The Conflict

Cllr Kestner is the Chair of the Audit and Risk Committee. Her declaration of interests reveals that she receives monthly rent payments directly from Lewisham Council via a Private Sector Leasing (PSL) Scheme.

The Reality

The individual legally appointed to audit the financial risks and management of the Council is concurrently acting as a private contractor receiving direct financial yields from the Council's housing department. She is financially dependent on the very authority she is tasked with regulating.

"The person appointed to audit the Council's finances is simultaneously on the Council's payroll as a private landlord. This is not a grey area — it is a structural failure."

II. The "Party-State" and Labour Relations

In a functioning local democracy, the Council acts as the employer, negotiating with trade unions who represent the employees. When the individuals sitting on the executive board of the Council are simultaneously employed by those same unions or the central political party, the neutrality required to manage public funds evaporates.

Part II — The Response

The Civic
Mandate

A Manifesto for Democratic Reclamation

Preamble. Local government exists to serve the community, protect public assets, and provide a safety net for the vulnerable. When the mechanisms of local democracy are captured by national party machinery, corporate developers, and private finance, the public interest is lost. We reject the normalisation of institutional capture, the "trickle-down" exploitation of our public land, and the silencing of local dissent.
I

Independence Over Party Loyalty

Local issues require local solutions, not national party dogma. We reject the use of the "Party Whip" in local council chambers, which stifles independent thought and forces representatives to vote against the interests of their own constituents. We support the election of independent, unwhipped representatives who answer only to the community, and we advocate for a shift to proportional representation to permanently end the "one-party state."

II

Community Wealth Over Corporate Extraction

Public land and public money must serve the public good. We reject economic models that hand our town centres to private equity while cutting front-line services. We advocate for Community Wealth Building: demanding that local institutions use their procurement budgets to support local businesses, worker co-operatives, and living-wage employers, ensuring wealth circulates within our community rather than leaking to corporate shareholders.

III

Distributed Power Over Executive Control

The concentration of power in the hands of a single Mayor or a tiny Cabinet breeds complacency and allows corporate lobbying to thrive in the shadows. We demand the dismantling of the "Strong Leader" model in favour of a modern Committee System, where decision-making is shared, transparent, and requires cross-party consensus. Furthermore, for decisions that permanently alter our borough, we demand the use of Citizens' Assemblies to put power directly into the hands of informed residents.

IV

Relentless Scrutiny Over Blind Trust

Transparency is not a request; it is a requirement. When auditors hold financial interests in the bodies they audit, the system has failed. We commit to the practice of open-source scrutiny: utilising Freedom of Information requests, scrutinising public registers, and exercising our legal right to inspect and object to local council accounts. We will not accept bureaucratic jargon as an excuse for financial conflicts of interest.

V

Collective Leverage Over Individual Complaints

When the council acts as an absentee landlord or ignores the needs of the vulnerable, traditional complaint forms are insufficient. We believe in the power of asymmetric direct action. We stand with Tenant Unions, community organisers, and crowdfunded legal challenges. When the internal democratic machinery is locked down, we will use our collective external leverage to force the council to the negotiating table.

The Pledge

We will no longer accept the managed decline of our public services masked by the shiny veneer of corporate real estate.

We commit to voting, organising, and investigating with one singular goal: to ensure our local government works in partnership with the people it represents, not the institutions that seek to profit from us.

This is the Civic Mandate.
